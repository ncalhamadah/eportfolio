Case Study

Facebook and Cambridge Analytica Data Breach (2014)


The Cambridge Analytica case is a significant example of the inappropriate use of surveys for data harvesting. Cambridge Analytica utilized personality quizzes on Facebook to collect data not only from participants but also from their friends, totaling data from up to 87 million users.

This data was then used to create psychographic profiles for targeted political advertising during the 2016 U.S. Presidential campaign​

Overview

Legalities

The case relates to data harvesting and manipulation of user privacy. According to Mark Zuckerberg, the consent policy is active; however, the enforcement of restrictions on misusing the data is not implemented. The act of passing data harvested from Facebook may violate the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in the EU.

Data Collection Policies


This case study highlights the need for strict consent, transparency, and limitations on where the gathered data will be used. In this case, it was a quiz to determine an individual's personality without transparently highlighting where the data would be used or the scope of the data collection activity. A policy consent to avoid claims of misuse can protect from hefty fines.

Active-active architecture is a system configuration in which multiple, geographically dispersed instances of a system simultaneously run and process data. Each instance in this configuration is fully functional and capable of taking over if the other fails. This enables uninterrupted service in the event of a system outage in one location, as the instance in the other location continues to handle requests without interruption. As shown in below illustration (provided by University of Essex):

Active-passive architecture is a system design strategy in which one system (the active) handles operations while a duplicate system (the passive) waits until the active fails.The passive system, usually distributed across multiple locations, mirrors the active system and is ready to take over operations at any time. If the active system fails, the passive system takes over, ensuring service. Disaster recovery and business continuity plans use this architecture for redundancy and resilience. Depending on how often the two systems sync, the failover from active to passive may take time.
‍
Cold standby systems are backups that are powered off or disconnected. This system only activates when the primary system fails or is down for maintenance.Disaster recovery is cost-effective with cold standby systems, which are usually located elsewhere. They allow system recovery but require significant downtime.The data on a cold standby system is usually backed up from the primary system at scheduled intervals, so data loss may occur between backups and system failure. Cold standby systems have higher RPO and RTO values than active-active or active-passive systems.

Independent Samples T Test - compare weight loss between diet A and B


The independent samples t-test was conducted to compare the mean weight loss between Diet A and Diet B. The F-test indicated no significant difference in variances (p=0.2699), allowing us to assume equal variances for the t-test. The results of the t-test revealed a significant difference in mean weight loss (p=0.0028), with Diet A showing a higher mean weight loss (5.34 kg) compared to Diet B (3.71 kg).

The estimated difference in means is 1.63 kg, suggesting that Diet A is more effective for weight loss. These findings strongly support the conclusion that Diet A leads to greater weight loss than Diet B.

Explanation of Independent Samples T-Test for Bank Cardholder Income
The objective is to determine whether the mean income of male bank cardholders is significantly different from that of female bank cardholders using an independent samples t-test.



In the findings, Variable 1 (males) has a higher mean income (52.91) compared to Variable 2 (females) with a mean income of 44.23. So, in terms of mean income, Variable 1 (males) appears to exceed more than females The p-value for the F-test (0.21824624) is greater than the significance level of 0.05, suggesting that there is no statistically significant difference in variances between the two groups. The p-value for the t-test (0.000709735) is much smaller than 0.05, indicating a statistically significant difference in mean income between the two groups.

Considering the focus is on the difference in means, and given that the t-test indicates a statistically significant difference in mean income between the two groups, we could argue that Variable 1 (males) is exceeds in terms of income compared to Variable 2 (females)
