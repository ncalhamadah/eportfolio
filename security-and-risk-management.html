Risk Management Process - Assesments
A procedure on identifying impacts of a risk includes two approaches which are Qualitative Assesment and Quantitative Assesment.

Qualitative Assesment
‍
The categorization of threats and vulnerabilities is the core of a qualitative risk assessment, which is typically employed when working with less valuable assets or in situations when the team lacks the knowledge or information necessary for a quantitative assessment. These are given designations like "high," "medium," or "low," depending on how likely or significant they are to occur.

This approach relies on descriptive categories to depict the possible danger rather than exact measures, giving a narrative comprehension of the threat landscape. Although largely descriptive, these categorical labels can eventually be converted into numerical values to aid in calculations like cost or attribution. Quantitative assessment

In risk management, it involves assigning numerical values to the likelihood and potential impact of risks. This approach uses historical data, industry benchmarks, and expert judgments to quantify risks. The resulting numerical values enable precise risk comparisons and data-driven decision-making. Monte Carlo Simulations (Predictive Analysis)

To support the basis for Quantitative Assesments, our class have tackled the use of AMonte Carlo simulation for predicting the probability and potential impact of various cyber threats in the context of cybersecurity. By incorporating a vast array of variables that reflect the inherent unpredictability of the digital landscape - such as the frequency of attacks, types of vulnerabilities, and efficacy of protective measures - the simulation generates a multitude of potential outcomes.B y running this simulation thousands or millions of times, a variety of possible scenarios are generated. Each scenario represents a potential system state following a cyberattack.

By analyzing the results, we can quantify the risk of particular disasters, enabling them to better prepare for, prevent, or mitigate these occurrences.

Security and Risk Standards in Industry and the Enterprise
After using the risk methods to identify threats that are present in company operations, there are standards to comply to for clients to build trust on their data privacy. There are data privacy laws to ensure the data of your clients/users are controllable and protected.

General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), is a data protection law that gives individuals more control over their personal data. It ensures that organizations handle personal data responsibly, obtain consent for its use, and protect it from breaches. GDPR aims to safeguard users' privacy rights, allowing them to access, correct, and delete their data while holding organizations accountable for proper data handling.

Does our mitigation strategy guarantee that we are robust enough to fulfill the regulations? Less worries thanks to Industry and enterprise security standards, they specify requirements and recommendations for managing security risks, protecting data, implementing controls, and ensuring legal and regulatory compliance. PCI-DSS - Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard

The Payment Card Industry Security Standards Council (PCI-SSC) has established the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI-DSS). This standard's primary objective is to combine and standardize the security practices and policies of Mastercard, Visa, American Express, Discover, and JCB. All entities that store, process, or transmit cardholder data and/or sensitive authentication data must comply with the PCI-DSS. The primary objective of the PCI-DSS is to reduce the risk of data breaches and improve the industry-wide security of payment card data. An important standard to be learned for safeguarding payments involves several objectives that need to be achieved. Disaster Recovery and Business Continuity

The subject has enumerated strategies to achieve lowest down-time of a business by implementing systems with the proper architecture to fulfill a RPO(Recovery Point Objective) and RTO (Recovery Time Objective) as low as 1-minute. In order to ensure High Availability (HA) for business continuity, the strategies include systems compatibility on multiple vendors to avoid situations such as vendor-lock ins.

RTO (Recovery Time Objective) is the targeted duration of time within which a business process must be restored after a disaster to avoid unacceptable consequences.

RPO (Recovery Point Objective) is the maximum targeted period in which data might be lost from an IT service due to a major incident.

Architectures has multiple approaches depending on company budget and needs, below are the types that are useful to ensure affordable downtimes avoiding disasters:
‍
RTO < 1hr ; RPO < 1m
RTO > 6hr; RPO > 15 mins.
RTO > 12hr ; Target RPO > 1hr.
RTO > 48hr ; RPO > 24hr.

Active-active architecture is a system configuration in which multiple, geographically dispersed instances of a system simultaneously run and process data. Each instance in this configuration is fully functional and capable of taking over if the other fails. This enables uninterrupted service in the event of a system outage in one location, as the instance in the other location continues to handle requests without interruption. As shown in below illustration (provided by University of Essex):
‍

Active-passive architecture is a system design strategy in which one system (the active) handles operations while a duplicate system (the passive) waits until the active fails.The passive system, usually distributed across multiple locations, mirrors the active system and is ready to take over operations at any time. If the active system fails, the passive system takes over, ensuring service. Disaster recovery and business continuity plans use this architecture for redundancy and resilience. Depending on how often the two systems sync, the failover from active to passive may take time.
‍
Cold standby systems are backups that are powered off or disconnected. This system only activates when the primary system fails or is down for maintenance.Disaster recovery is cost-effective with cold standby systems, which are usually located elsewhere. They allow system recovery but require significant downtime.The data on a cold standby system is usually backed up from the primary system at scheduled intervals, so data loss may occur between backups and system failure. Cold standby systems have higher RPO and RTO values than active-active or active-passive systems.
A straightforward and affordable disaster recovery strategy is backup and restore. It entails periodically producing data backups that are kept in a backup location. This backup data is used to restore the system in the event of data loss or system failure, preserving business operations. Although this approach might lead to higher Recovery Time Objectives (RTO) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPO), it is still a desirable option for businesses that don't require complex systems because of its affordability and simplicity.
